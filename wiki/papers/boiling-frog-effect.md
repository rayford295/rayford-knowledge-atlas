## Summary

This article studies how global warming changes the emotional impacts of air pollution. It uses large-scale geotagged Twitter sentiment across the continental United States to show that combined heatwaves and air pollution can produce delayed declines in expressed sentiment, especially in warmer climates.

## Method Snapshot

The study combines more than 850 million geotagged tweets, air pollution exposure, heatwave indicators, precipitation, climate zones, and county-level controls from 2016 to 2022. It uses spatiotemporal Bayesian hierarchical models with distributed lag non-linear functions to estimate where and when environmental stressors affect public sentiment.

## Data and Study Area

The study covers 3,144 counties in the 48 contiguous U.S. states during summer months from 2016 to 2022. Sentiment is aggregated from geotagged tweets at the county-day level and linked to daily environmental exposure data.

## How This Connects to My Other Work

This paper connects the atlas branch on public signals to climate risk and environmental health. It extends earlier work on online public emotion by treating sentiment as a near-real-time spatial signal for compound environmental stress.

## Impact

The paper adds a climate-emotion node to the research graph. It shows that warmer regions may adapt to heat in ways that delay emotional responses to worsening air quality, which matters for public health warning systems, environmental governance, and place-based climate adaptation.
